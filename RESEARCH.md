## 1. The "AI Candidate" / AI-Powered Governance Landscape (2024-2026)

### Real Campaigns

**AI Steve (UK, 2024)**
Steve Endacott, chairman of Neural Voice, ran for UK Parliament in Brighton Pavilion in the July 2024 general election. "AI Steve" was an LLM-powered avatar that constituents could talk to 24/7 on ai-steve.co.uk. The system collected voter opinions and fed them to a panel of "validators" who scored policies weekly. Policies scoring above 50% became official platform positions. Endacott described himself as "the real politician going into Parliament, but controlled by my co-pilot." He received 179 votes (0.3%). Party: Smarter U.K. (not registered in time, ran as independent).

Key quote: "It's not AI taking over the world. It's AI being used as a technical way of connecting to our constituents and reinventing democracy by saying, 'You don't just vote for somebody every four years; you actually control the vote on an ongoing basis.'" (NBC News, June 13, 2024)

Sources:
- https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/tech-news/ai-candidate-running-parliament-uk-says-ai-can-humanize-politics-rcna156991
- https://www.independent.co.uk/news/uk/politics/election-politics-uk-ai-steve-brighton-b2559777.html
- https://www.reuters.com/world/uk/no-hallucination-ai-candidate-ballot-uk-election-2024-06-19/

**VIC / Victor Miller (Cheyenne, Wyoming, 2024)**
Victor Miller ran for mayor of Cheyenne with a ChatGPT-based chatbot called VIC (Virtual Integrated Citizen). His campaign promise: VIC would make the decisions, not him. Miller claimed VIC had "better ideas and a better grasp of the law than many people currently serving in government." He lost the race (August 2024).

Sources:
- https://www.wired.com/story/ai-bot-running-for-mayor-wyoming/
- https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/article/2024/aug/21/wyoming-cheyenne-ai-bot-mayor

**Jay Jack (2024)**
Another AI-adjacent candidate. Limited information available. Website: jayjack2024.com.

**Denmark's Synthetic Party (2022)**
A Danish political party founded on an AI-derived platform. The party's policies were generated by AI trained on fringe Danish party platforms since 1970. The AI leader was called "Leader Lars." Never won seats but generated significant media attention.

Source: https://www.vice.com/en/article/jgpb3p/this-danish-political-party-is-led-by-an-ai

### Governance AI Projects (Not Campaigns)

**MARIA OS (2025-2026)**
A framework for "Pausable Policy Design" (PPD) in municipal AI. Formalizes how government AI policies can be paused, evaluated, and controlled. Models policies as state machines with pause conditions and accountability requirements. Academic/theoretical but relevant to OpenCandidate's transparency goals.

Source: https://os.maria-code.ai/en/blog/pausable-policy-design-municipal

**Mayor.town (2025)**
Advocates for on-premise municipal AI systems for small-town governance. AI trained on town-specific data, operates offline, supports emergency decision-making. Governed by local officials. Not a campaign but a governance-focused project.

Source: https://mayor.town/on-premise-ai/

### Public Reaction Patterns

From the coverage, public reaction to AI candidates clusters into three buckets:

1. **Curiosity/novelty** (most common): People engage with the AI, test its limits, treat it as entertainment. AI Steve got ~1,000 calls in one night after news leaked on X.
2. **Skepticism about motives**: "Is this a tech company marketing stunt?" Endacott had to repeatedly insist it wasn't a joke and disclaim his Neural Voice stake.
3. **Philosophical concern**: Academics and commentators worry about accountability. The Conversation ran a piece testing AI Steve and finding it formulaic. The Guardian framed the Wyoming race around democratic legitimacy.

Nobody has yet combined all three ingredients: (a) a real candidate, (b) transparent AI reasoning, and (c) a structured commitment framework. That's the OpenCandidate gap.

## 2. Political Pledge & Commitment Frameworks

### The ATR Model (Gold Standard for Political Pledges)

Americans for Tax Reform's Taxpayer Protection Pledge is the most successful political commitment device in modern American politics. Key design elements:

- **Simple, specific language**: "I will oppose and vote against any and all efforts to increase taxes." One sentence. No wiggle room.
- **Written signature**: Candidates physically sign the pledge. The signed document is public.
- **Public database**: ATR maintains a searchable database of who signed and who didn't (atr.org/pledge-database/).
- **Enforcement via shame**: Breaking the pledge triggers ATR attacks and primary challenges. George H.W. Bush's "read my lips" reversal is the canonical cautionary tale.
- **Bipartisan visibility**: Even candidates who refuse to sign get asked about it, making the pledge a framing device.

Source: https://www.atr.org/about-the-pledge/

### What Makes Political Commitments Credible?

From ATR and other pledge frameworks (term limits, climate pledges), the credibility factors are:

1. **Specificity**: Vague commitments ("I'll fight for working families") are worthless. Specific, falsifiable commitments work.
2. **Public record**: The commitment must be documented and searchable. A signed PDF on a public website beats a verbal promise.
3. **Third-party monitoring**: Someone other than the candidate tracks compliance. ATR, climate watchdogs, etc.
4. **Cost of defection**: Breaking the pledge must have consequences. Social, electoral, or reputational.
5. **Simplicity**: If you can't explain the pledge in one sentence, it won't stick.

### Proposed "OpenCandidate Pledge" Design

Based on the above patterns, an OpenCandidate Pledge could work as follows:

> "I pledge to publish the full reasoning chain of any AI system I use in policy analysis or decision-making, within 72 hours of acting on its output, on a publicly accessible platform."

Design elements to borrow:
- Physical/digital signature, publicly recorded
- OpenCandidate maintains the compliance database
- Graded compliance (full transparency, partial, none) rather than binary
- Quarterly "transparency scorecards" published by OpenCandidate
- Any constituent can submit a FOIA-style "reasoning request" for a specific decision

## 3. Digital Democracy / Deliberative Democracy Platforms

### Pol.is

- Open-source platform for large-scale opinion gathering
- Uses real-time machine learning to cluster participants into opinion groups
- No reply button (by design). Users submit short statements, others vote agree/disagree/pass
- Surfaces consensus and divides simultaneously
- Used by vTaiwan, UK government, Seattle, others
- Key insight: removes the "loudest voice wins" dynamic of forums/town halls
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pol.is, https://compdemocracy.org

**Relevance to OpenCandidate**: Pol.is-style opinion clustering could power the "top questions" feature. Instead of upvoting questions (which favors early posts), Pol.is surfaces questions that cut across opinion groups. This produces more interesting, less partisan question sets.

### vTaiwan

- Taiwan's multi-stakeholder consultation platform, launched 2014
- Created by Audrey Tang (Taiwan's Digital Minister) and g0v civic hackers
- Process: (1) open proposal, (2) Pol.is opinion gathering, (3) in-person stakeholder meetings, (4) government response
- Used for ride-sharing regulation (UberX), fintech sandbox, telemedicine, alcohol sales
- 26 cases completed as of 2023, with ~80% leading to government action
- Source: https://info.vtaiwan.tw/, https://democracy-technologies.org/participation/consensus-building-in-taiwan

**Key lesson**: vTaiwan works because it has a formal pipeline from citizen input to government action. It's not just a suggestion box. OpenCandidate should similarly define what happens after questions are surfaced.

### Decidim

- Open-source participatory democracy platform, created by Barcelona City Council (2016)
- Used by 400+ organizations in 20+ countries
- Modular: supports proposals, debates, meetings, participatory budgets, surveys, sortition
- Barcelona used it for its strategic plan: 40,000 proposals from 70,000 participants
- Source: https://decidim.org/

**Relevance**: Decidim's modular architecture is a good reference for OpenCandidate's platform design. Especially the participatory budgeting and proposal modules.

### Better Reykjavik

- Iceland's online citizen participation platform, launched 2010 by Citizens Foundation
- Citizens submit ideas, debate pros/cons, vote
- Reykjavik city council reviews top-voted ideas monthly
- Over 70,000 users (in a city of ~130,000), 17,000+ ideas submitted
- ~700 ideas formally considered by city council
- Recognized by OECD as innovation in public sector
- Source: https://citizens.is/portfolio_page/better_reykjavik, https://oecd-opsi.org/innovations/better-reykjavik/

**Key lesson**: Better Reykjavik proves that sustained civic participation is possible at meaningful scale. The city formally committed to reviewing top ideas, creating a feedback loop. Without the feedback loop, participation dies.

### Summary Table: What to Learn from Each

| Platform | Key Innovation | OpenCandidate Lesson |
|---|---|---|
| Pol.is | ML-driven opinion clustering, no reply threads | Use for surfacing top questions without letting partisans dominate |
| vTaiwan | Formal pipeline from input to government action | Define what happens after questions are answered |
| Decidim | Modular, open-source, 400+ deployments | Architecture reference; don't reinvent the wheel |
| Better Reykjavik | Sustained participation, monthly review commitment | Prove the feedback loop works to sustain engagement |

## 4. Legal Considerations

### Federal Law: No Specific Prohibition on AI-Assisted Decision-Making

The CRS Insight (IN12222, updated September 25, 2024) states plainly:

> "No federal statute or regulation specifically addresses artificial intelligence (AI) in political campaigns. ... Campaign management decisions, such as which technology to use, are generally not subject to regulation."

A candidate is free to use any technology for decision-making. The legal landscape focuses entirely on **deceptive AI content in campaign advertising**, not on AI-assisted governance or policy analysis.

Source: https://www.everycrsreport.com/files/2024-09-25_IN12222_99d92d5b4f262d7e1aaa8ca2178f9c82d4992558.html

### FEC Activity (2024-2025)

The FEC has been active on AI in campaign **ads**, not AI in governance:

- **September 2024**: FEC published Agenda Document 24-39-A on AI in campaign ads
- **2024-2025**: FEC sought public comments on amending regulations to address "deliberately deceptive artificial intelligence in campaign ads"
- **2025**: FEC approved an interpretive rule on AI in campaign ads, focusing on disclosure requirements
- FEC's focus is entirely on deepfakes and synthetic media in **advertising**. Using AI for policy analysis or decision support is outside their purview.

Sources:
- https://www.fec.gov/resources/cms-content/documents/mtgdoc-24-39-A.pdf
- https://fec.gov/updates/commission-approves-notification-of-disposition-interpretive-rule-on-artificial-intelligence-in-campaign-ads/

### State-Level Activity

Multiple states have passed or proposed laws on AI in campaigns, but again focused on deepfakes/synthetic media in ads:

- Over a dozen bills introduced in the 118th Congress (H.R. 3044, H.R. 3106, S. 686, S. 3875, etc.)
- S. 3875 (reported by Senate Rules Committee, May 2024) would amend FECA to require disclaimers on AI-generated political ads
- None of these address AI-assisted governance or decision-making

### Professional Standards

The American Association of Political Consultants (AAPC) unanimously condemned "use of deceptive generative AI content in political campaigns" in May 2023. This is a voluntary code of ethics, not regulation, and again focuses on deception in content, not transparent use of AI for analysis.

### Legal Bottom Line for OpenCandidate

1. **No legal barrier** to a candidate using AI for decision-making or policy analysis
2. **No legal requirement** to disclose AI use in governance (which makes a voluntary pledge more powerful)
3. The legal landscape is entirely focused on preventing **deceptive AI content** in campaign advertising
4. A candidate who voluntarily commits to AI transparency is operating in legal white space. There's nothing to violate and nothing requiring it. The pledge is purely a trust mechanism.
5. If OpenCandidate candidates publish AI reasoning chains, they should ensure those don't contain material that could be construed as campaign communications requiring FEC disclaimers (edge case but worth noting)

## 5. Strategic Implications for OpenCandidate

### The Gap in the Market

Nobody has combined:
- A real candidate who commits to AI transparency (pledge)
- A platform for citizens to submit and rank questions (digital democracy)
- Public AI reasoning chains (transparency)
- Third-party monitoring of compliance (accountability)

AI Steve came closest but lacked the transparency component (voters couldn't see the AI's reasoning, just its outputs). VIC/Victor Miller was a stunt with no accountability framework. The Danish Synthetic Party was art, not politics.

### What OpenCandidate Can Learn from Failures

1. **AI Steve got 179 votes.** The novelty wasn't enough. OpenCandidate needs to be a serious governance tool, not a tech demo.
2. **Victor Miller lost.** "The AI will make all decisions" is a losing message. "I use AI to analyze policy and show you my reasoning" is different.
3. **Denmark's Synthetic Party** was never serious. OpenCandidate must avoid the art-project framing.

### Recommended Positioning

OpenCandidate is not about AI replacing politicians. It's about radical transparency in how decisions get made. The AI is a tool; the transparency is the product. The pledge is the commitment device. The platform is where citizens hold candidates accountable.

This positions OpenCandidate as a governance reform project, not an AI novelty. The comparison set should be vTaiwan and Better Reykjavik, not AI Steve.
